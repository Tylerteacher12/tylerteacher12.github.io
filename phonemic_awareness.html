Phonemic Awareness: The Key to Understanding Spoken English

Tyler Mar 5 2022

Don’t understand what you are hearing in English? Improve your phonemic awareness

“I can read and write in English, and I have an excellent vocabulary. I’ve been learning for years, but sometimes I really don’t understand what I am hearing.”

Does this sound like you? Don’t worry, you are not alone! Many students learn English from textbooks. Many more students start out with exam scores as their main motivation, and never have the opportunity to interact with native speakers. They learn words and syntax, but listening skills are often ignored.

From classrooms to language apps like DuoLingo, phonemic awareness is largely ignored. What exactly is “phonemic awareness''? According to ReadingRockets.org: "Phonemic awareness is the ability to identify and manipulate individual sounds (phonemes) in spoken words. " In English, the sounds don’t always correspond to the spelling–making recognizing different sounds especially difficult.

How can we improve our phonemic awareness?

Identify the sounds you are struggling with

There is a good chance that some of the sounds in English do not exist in your native language. This can cause difficulties not only in pronunciation, but it can also cause us not to recognize the sounds that we are hearing.

For example, there are five vowel sounds in Spanish; in contrast, English has between fourteen and twenty different vowel sounds–depending on the dialect. This can cause native Spanish speakers to confuse many words that only differ by a single vowel.

If you want to learn what sounds exist in English but don’t exist in your native language, the answer is a quick Google search away. Try searching “common pronunciation errors by [Spanish] speakers in English” or “What english phonemes don't exist in [Turkish]” to learn more.

Listening Discrimination Drills

Like all skills, you can learn phonemic awareness through deliberate practice. There are a number of ways to do this. Once you have identified the sounds that you are struggling with, there are a number of games and activities that you can use to practice.

Minimal pair activities are simple drills that will help you to recognize the difference in different sounds in English. You will need a partner for this activity–or you can probably find a virtual partner on YouTube.

First, choose two sounds that are often confused for one another. Pay attention to how it feels to make these sounds. Every sound is made at a different position in the mouth. How are these sounds different from each other?

Next, make two columns of words that differ only by these sounds. If we were practicing the long /e/ and short /i/ sounds, we might use words like ‘sheep, bleep, cheap’ in one column, and ‘ship, blip, chip’ in the other.

Finally, listen to the words and see if you can identify which sound you are hearing. This will get easier with practice.

There are many ways to do this activity, so get creative! Turn it into a game and practice with a friend.

Poetry is a friend of good phonemic awareness

Poems are one of the best resources for improving our listening skills. Many poems use rhyme schemes that rely on the same sound across two or more lines of poetry. These rhymes aren’t always easy to hear for non-native speakers.

The internet is full of poems, both written down and being read-aloud. Poetry.org is a great resource for finding poems, and so is YouTube. Spend some time listening to poems to see if you can recognize the rhymes. If aren’t sure if two words rhyme, you can google “what rhymes with ….” for lists of all words that rhyme with the word you are curious about.

Intonation can be another factor that makes understanding English difficult. Intonation is the tone, speed and rhythm of a language. We can think of intonation as the “music” of language. All languages have their own intonation.

Poets love to play with intonation. Listening to poems and trying to match the intonation used by the person reading it is a great way to improve our own intonation when speaking. It is also a good way to make the intonation of a language more familiar when you are listening.
